# 1. Mission, constraints, and the correct research question

The goal is to teach a beginner how to look for possible alpha using only OHLCV data. In this document, alpha does not mean a chart pattern that looks good. It means a repeatable conditional behavior that survives realistic statistical checks, out-of-sample testing, costs, and robustness analysis.

The starting dataset is one time-ordered table with timestamp, open, high, low, close, and volume. Everything else must be derived from those fields. This means the framework cannot assume the instrument is a stock, future, currency, crypto asset, index, ETF, bond, spread, synthetic asset, or anything else. It also cannot assume regular exchange hours unless the timestamp structure itself proves that such sessions exist.

The correct beginner question is not: which indicator predicts price? The correct question is: when price, range, volume, or candle structure is unusual relative to its own recent history, does the future distribution of returns, ranges, or paths change in a statistically meaningful and tradeable way?

> **Core principle**
> Do not begin with a strategy. Begin with conditional distributions. A strategy is only created after an anomaly survives event-study testing, regime splits, threshold robustness, data-snooping controls, and cost stress.

## 1.1 The closed OHLCV information set

Define the information available at the end of bar t as F\_t, the sigma-field generated by all OHLCV bars from the beginning of the sample through bar t. A valid signal at bar t must be F\_t-measurable. In plain English: the signal can only use data that was already known at that time.

```text
Allowed at time t:
    open_1..open_t, high_1..high_t, low_1..low_t, close_1..close_t, volume_1..volume_t

Not allowed at time t:
    close_{t+1}, high_{t+1}, future rolling means, future volatility, revised labels,
    full-sample means, full-sample standard deviations, hindsight-selected regimes.
```

This formal rule is the cleanest way to prevent lookahead bias. Every feature, threshold, rolling statistic, regime label, and signal must be computable from the past and present only.

## 1.2 What counts as alpha in this document

A conditional anomaly becomes an alpha candidate only if at least one future target changes relative to baseline. The target can be directional return, absolute return, future range, skew, probability of hitting a barrier first, or drawdown-adjusted path quality.

```text
A_t = an event condition known at bar t
r_{t,h} = ln(close_{t+h} / close_t)

Directional edge:
    Edge_h(A) = E[r_{t,h} | A_t] - E[r_{t,h}]

Volatility edge:
    VolEdge_h(A) = E[abs(r_{t,h}) | A_t] / E[abs(r_{t,h})]

Path edge:
    PathEdge(A) = P(up_barrier first | A_t) - P(up_barrier first baseline)
```

The anomaly does not need to forecast direction. A signal that forecasts future movement magnitude can be valuable for breakout timing, volatility targeting, option-like structures, or deciding when not to trade. Since this document avoids instrument-specific assumptions, it treats directional alpha and volatility alpha separately.

## 1.3 The research posture

- Skeptical by default: most apparent edges are noise until they survive out-of-sample validation.
- Instrument-agnostic: every feature must scale across price levels, volatility levels, and volume regimes.
- Distribution-first: inspect the full distribution, not only the average future return.
- Costs-last but costs-required: discover gross statistical structure first, then stress it across a wide cost grid.
- Log everything: every tested idea is part of the multiple-testing burden, even if you do not like the result.

# 2. Research principles from the literature

This framework is practical, but its validation discipline is informed by a few important research lines. Lo, Mamaysky, and Wang showed that technical-pattern research can be turned into a systematic statistical problem rather than a purely visual exercise [1]. White formalized the danger of data snooping when the same dataset is reused for model discovery and inference [3]. Sullivan, Timmermann, and White applied that logic to technical trading rules [2]. Hansen improved the data-snooping test through the Superior Predictive Ability framework [4]. Bailey, Borwein, Lopez de Prado, and Zhu emphasized the probability of backtest overfitting when many historical simulations are tried [5], and Bailey and Lopez de Prado proposed the Deflated Sharpe Ratio to correct for selection bias and non-normal returns [6].

The implication for a beginner is simple: finding an impressive backtest is easy; finding one that is not a statistical accident is hard. A serious OHLCV alpha workflow therefore needs to treat the research process itself as a source of bias.

| Research issue | Why it matters for OHLCV alpha discovery | Framework response |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Technical-pattern subjectivity | Visual pattern matching can become hindsight storytelling. | Convert every idea into explicit formulas, event conditions, and forward distributions. |
| Data snooping | Testing many indicators and thresholds creates false positives. | Track all trials; use validation sets, Reality Check/SPA ideas, FDR control, and strong out-of-sample standards. |
| Backtest overfitting | The best in-sample rule may simply be the best noise fit. | Use walk-forward validation, threshold robustness, de-duplicated events, and cost stress. |
| Serial dependence | Financial returns and signal returns are not clean IID samples. | Use non-overlapping events, HAC/Newey-West style inference, or block/stationary bootstrap. |
| Non-normal returns | Mean/std assumptions can hide skew, fat tails, and crash risk. | Inspect quantiles, skew, kurtosis, tail loss, MFE/MAE, and barrier outcomes. |
| Range information | OHLC data contains more intrabar information than close-to-close returns. | Use true range, Parkinson, Garman-Klass, Rogers-Satchell, and Yang-Zhang-style estimators as feature candidates. |

The document does not claim these papers prove any particular OHLCV anomaly works. Instead, they justify the research discipline: formal definitions, robust inference, honest treatment of multiple testing, and conservative validation.

# 3. Formal alpha definition using only past OHLCV information

Let each bar be indexed by t. Let O\_t, H\_t, L\_t, C\_t, and V\_t be open, high, low, close, and volume. The full bar is X\_t = (O\_t, H\_t, L\_t, C\_t, V\_t). A feature f\_t is valid only if f\_t = g(X\_1, X\_2, ..., X\_t) for some deterministic function g.

```text
Valid feature examples:
    ln(C_t / C_{t-1})
    rolling_mean(ln(C_i / C_{i-1}), i=t-100..t-1)
    rolling_high_20 = max(H_{t-20},...,H_{t-1})
    volume_z_t = (ln(1+V_t) - median_past_200) / MAD_past_200

Invalid feature examples:
    full_sample_z = (r_t - mean(r_1..r_T)) / std(r_1..r_T)
    future_regime = label created using C_{t+20}
    breakout using max(H_{t-20}..H_t) when deciding at the same close.
```

A signal is a Boolean or real-valued function of valid features. An event study asks whether the future target distribution after the signal differs from its baseline distribution. In a rigorous workflow, the signal definition is chosen on training data, maybe tuned on validation data, and judged once on an untouched test segment.

## 3.1 The three alpha types you can search for from OHLCV only

| Alpha type | Target | Example condition | Possible use |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Directional alpha | Future log return fwd\_r\_h | Failed upside breakout with high volume | Long/short signal after validation |
| Volatility alpha | Future absolute return or future high-low range | ATR compression percentile below 10% | Timing movement, breakout watchlist, volatility regime filter |
| Path alpha | Probability of hitting up/down barrier first; MFE/MAE | Large lower wick after downside shock | Stop/target design and signal tradability analysis |

Separating these alpha types prevents a common beginner mistake: discarding a condition because mean future return is zero even though future realized range is unusually large, or trusting a positive mean while ignoring unacceptable adverse excursion.

# 4. Data engineering and cleaning protocol

Bad data creates fake alpha. Before any anomaly search, make the bar table internally consistent. Data cleaning should be deterministic, documented, and done before feature generation. Never silently repair suspicious bars unless the repair rule is fixed in advance.

## 4.1 Required columns and integrity checks

| Check | Rule | Action |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Timestamp ordering | timestamps must be strictly increasing after sorting | sort; remove or inspect duplicates |
| Positive prices | open, high, low, close must be > 0 | drop or flag invalid rows |
| Volume nonnegative | volume must be >= 0 | drop, set missing to NaN, or flag depending on source |
| OHLC consistency | high >= max(open, close, low) and low <= min(open, close, high) | flag impossible bars; do not normalize them away without audit |
| Missing intervals | expected timestamp grid has gaps | segment data, insert flat bars only if zero-trade periods are valid, or exclude from rolling windows |
| Duplicate timestamps | same timestamp appears more than once | aggregate only if source semantics are known; otherwise remove duplicates |
| Extreme bad ticks | single-bar returns/ranges far beyond plausible local history | flag for sensitivity tests rather than automatic deletion |

## 4.2 Missing-candle policy

Because the framework cannot assume a market session, missing bars must be handled from the timestamp grid itself. Choose one policy and keep it fixed for the whole research run.

| Policy | How it works | When it is safer |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Drop gaps | Remove missing intervals and compute rolling features only on observed bars. | Gaps are rare and not meaningful. |
| Insert flat bars | open=high=low=close=previous close, volume=0. | The bar interval existed but there was genuinely no activity. |
| Segment data | Break the series into continuous blocks and reset rolling features at segment boundaries. | Safest default when gaps may reflect closures, outages, or missing source data. |

## 4.3 Clean-data pseudocode

```python
def clean_ohlcv(df, timestamp_col='timestamp'):
    required = ['open', 'high', 'low', 'close', 'volume']
    assert all(c in df.columns for c in required)

    df = df.sort_values(timestamp_col).drop_duplicates(timestamp_col, keep='last').copy()

    price_ok = (df[['open','high','low','close']] > 0).all(axis=1)
    volume_ok = df['volume'].fillna(-1).ge(0)
    structure_ok = (
        df['high'].ge(df[['open','close','low']].max(axis=1)) &
        df['low'].le(df[['open','close','high']].min(axis=1))
    )

    df['bad_bar'] = ~(price_ok & volume_ok & structure_ok)
    return df.loc[~df['bad_bar']].copy()
```

> **Reasoning rule**
> A data-cleaning choice is part of the strategy research. If the alpha only exists after aggressive deletion of inconvenient bars, assume the alpha is suspect until proven otherwise.

# 5. Core mathematical transformations

Use log-space as the default because log returns are scale-normalized and additive across time. Raw price differences are generally not comparable across price levels. Most OHLC features should be expressed as log ratios or normalized ratios.

| Feature | Formula | Interpretation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Close return | r1\_t = ln(C\_t / C\_{t-1}) | One-bar log return. |
| Multi-bar return | rN\_t = ln(C\_t / C\_{t-N}) | Net movement over N bars. |
| Open-to-close | oc\_t = ln(C\_t / O\_t) | Direction inside the bar. |
| Gap | gap\_t = ln(O\_t / C\_{t-1}) | Open jump from previous close. |
| High-low range | hl\_t = ln(H\_t / L\_t) | Intrabar realized range. |
| True range log | tr\_t = max(ln(H\_t/L\_t), abs(ln(H\_t/C\_{t-1})), abs(ln(L\_t/C\_{t-1}))) | Range including gap against prior close. |
| Body | body\_t = abs(ln(C\_t / O\_t)) | Absolute open-close movement. |
| Upper wick | uw\_t = ln(H\_t / max(O\_t, C\_t)) | Rejection or excursion above the body. |
| Lower wick | lw\_t = ln(min(O\_t, C\_t) / L\_t) | Rejection or excursion below the body. |
| Close location value | clv\_t = (2*C\_t - H\_t - L\_t) / (H\_t - L\_t) | Close near high is +1; close near low is -1. |

For any formula with H\_t = L\_t, define range-normalized ratios safely. For example, set CLV to 0 when the candle has zero range, or mark it missing and let later filters decide.

## 5.1 Conservative rolling normalization

The safest way to normalize a current feature x\_t is to compare it to the distribution of prior observations, not to a full-sample distribution and not to a window that includes future values.

```text
mean_prev_n = x.shift(1).rolling(n).mean()
std_prev_n  = x.shift(1).rolling(n).std()
z_t = (x_t - mean_prev_n) / std_prev_n

median_prev_n = x.shift(1).rolling(n).median()
mad_prev_n    = median(abs(x_i - median_prev_n), i=t-n..t-1)
robust_z_t = 0.6745 * (x_t - median_prev_n) / mad_prev_n
```

Including the current value in the denominator is not future leakage, but it can dampen the apparent extremeness of the current observation. Excluding the current value gives a cleaner anomaly interpretation: how strange is this bar relative to what was known before it printed?

# 6. Full OHLCV feature library

The feature library should be broad enough to cover different statistical behaviors but not so chaotic that every result is a data-mined accident. Start with features that have clear definitions and clear hypotheses.

## 6.1 Return features

| Name | Formula | Use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| r1 | ln(C\_t / C\_{t-1}) | Net log return over 1 bars. |
| r2 | ln(C\_t / C\_{t-2}) | Net log return over 2 bars. |
| r3 | ln(C\_t / C\_{t-3}) | Net log return over 3 bars. |
| r5 | ln(C\_t / C\_{t-5}) | Net log return over 5 bars. |
| r10 | ln(C\_t / C\_{t-10}) | Net log return over 10 bars. |
| r20 | ln(C\_t / C\_{t-20}) | Net log return over 20 bars. |
| r50 | ln(C\_t / C\_{t-50}) | Net log return over 50 bars. |
| r100 | ln(C\_t / C\_{t-100}) | Net log return over 100 bars. |
| abs\_rN | abs(rN) | Movement magnitude without direction. |
| signed\_abs\_ratio | rN / mean(abs(r1), n) | Return scaled by recent average absolute move. |
| return\_accel | r\_short - r\_long * short/long | Short-term move relative to slower move. |
| return\_rank | percentile\_rank(r1\_t, prior n) | Nonparametric extremeness of current return. |

## 6.2 Range and volatility features

| Name | Formula | Use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| hl\_range | ln(H/L) | Basic intrabar range. |
| true\_range\_log | max(ln(H/L), abs(ln(H/C\_prev)), abs(ln(L/C\_prev))) | Gap-aware bar range. |
| ATR\_log\_n | mean(true\_range\_log, n) | Rolling range-volatility proxy. |
| realized\_vol\_n | sqrt(sum(r1^2, n)) | Close-to-close realized volatility. |
| mean\_abs\_return\_n | mean(abs(r1), n) | Robust-ish movement scale. |
| vol\_ratio | ATR\_log\_short / ATR\_log\_long | Volatility compression or expansion. |
| range\_z | zscore(hl\_range, prior n) | Range anomaly score. |
| range\_percentile | rank(hl\_range\_t within prior n) | Distribution-free range anomaly. |
| Parkinson\_var | (1/(4*ln(2))) * ln(H/L)^2 | High-low variance estimator candidate. |
| Garman\_Klass\_var | 0.5*ln(H/L)^2 - (2*ln(2)-1)*ln(C/O)^2 | OHLC variance estimator candidate. |
| Rogers\_Satchell | ln(H/C)*ln(H/O) + ln(L/C)*ln(L/O) | Drift-robust OHLC volatility component. |
| Yang\_Zhang\_style | gap var + k*open-close var + (1-k)*Rogers-Satchell | Gap-aware OHLC volatility framework. |

The named range-based estimators are not trading rules by themselves. They are volatility features. The range-based volatility literature exists because high, low, open, and close can contain more information about intrabar variation than close-to-close returns alone [12]-[15].

| doji\_like | body\_ratio < threshold | Small body relative to range. |

| Feature | Formula | Use |

# 7. Normalization, stationarity, and anomaly thresholds

An anomaly is not a large raw number. An anomaly is a value that is unusually large or small relative to a relevant historical baseline that would have been known at the time. Use multiple normalization methods because financial data is fat-tailed, non-stationary, and regime-dependent.

| Method | Formula | Strength | Weakness |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Rolling z-score | (x\_t - mean\_past\_n) / std\_past\_n | Simple and interpretable. | Sensitive to outliers and non-normality. |
| Robust z-score | 0.6745*(x\_t - median\_past\_n)/MAD\_past\_n | Better for fat tails and volume spikes. | MAD can be zero in inactive data. |
| Percentile rank | rank of x\_t among prior n observations | Distribution-free and beginner friendly. | Less sensitive to magnitude beyond top percentile. |
| Ratio to median | x\_t / median\_past\_n | Intuitive for positive features like volume/range. | Unstable if median is near zero. |
| Volatility scaling | return / ATR or return / std | Compares movement to current scale. | Volatility estimate itself can lag. |

## 7.1 Threshold design

Start with broad, round-number thresholds. A real phenomenon should not depend on a threshold like 2.137. Use threshold sweeps to see whether the sign and magnitude of the edge change smoothly.

| Feature type | Moderate anomaly | Strong anomaly | Extreme anomaly |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| z-score | abs(z) > 1.5 | abs(z) > 2.0 | abs(z) > 3.0 |
| percentile high | > 0.80 | > 0.95 | > 0.99 |
| percentile low | < 0.20 | < 0.05 | < 0.01 |
| volatility ratio | < 0.75 or > 1.25 | < 0.60 or > 1.50 | < 0.40 or > 2.00 |
| efficiency | < 0.25 or > 0.55 | < 0.20 or > 0.65 | < 0.10 or > 0.80 |

## 7.2 Robustness principle

A candidate anomaly should survive small changes in lookback length, threshold, target horizon, and event de-duplication rule. If a signal works only at one exact parameter combination, assume it is a curve fit until out-of-sample results prove otherwise.

```text
Example robustness grid:
    lookback_n:       50, 100, 200, 500
    z_threshold:      1.5, 2.0, 2.5, 3.0
    percentile:       0.80, 0.90, 0.95, 0.99
    horizon_h:        1, 3, 5, 10, 20
    cooldown_h:       horizon_h or 2*horizon_h
    cost_bps:         0, 1, 2, 5, 10, 20, 50
```

# 8. Anomaly taxonomy

The taxonomy below is the practical search map. Each anomaly is a condition at time t. The research question is always: after this condition, how does the future distribution change?

## 8.1 Return shock anomalies

Return shocks are unusually large positive or negative close-to-close or open-to-close moves. They can indicate momentum, reversal, volatility expansion, or exhaustion. Test both direction and absolute movement.

```python
Bullish return shock:
    r1_z_100 > +2

Bearish return shock:
    r1_z_100 < -2

Symmetric shock:
    abs(r1_z_100) > 2

Questions:
    Does the move continue?
    Does it reverse?
    Does it only predict larger future range?
    Does volume decide continuation versus reversal?
```

## 8.2 Range expansion anomalies

Range expansion means the high-low or true range is unusually large. This often changes future volatility more reliably than future direction, so test fwd\_abs\_return and future\_range in addition to fwd\_return.

```text
Large range:
    range_z_100 > 2
    or range_percentile_200 > 0.95

Directional large range:
    range_z_100 > 2 and clv > 0.8
    range_z_100 > 2 and clv < -0.8

Questions:
    Is the next move continuation, reversal, or simply high-volatility?
```

## 8.3 Range compression anomalies

Range compression means movement is unusually small. Compression is often a setup, not a direction. The immediate target should be future movement magnitude.

```text
Compression:
    atr_10 / atr_100 < 0.6
    or range_percentile_100 < 0.10

Targets:
    fwd_abs_r5, fwd_abs_r10, future_range_10

Then add trigger:
    compression followed by upside breakout
    compression followed by downside breakout
```

## 8.4 Volume shock anomalies

Volume shock means participation is abnormal relative to recent history. Volume alone is ambiguous. The useful information usually comes from volume combined with return, range, candle body, or close location.

```text
Volume shock:
    volume_z_200 > 2
    or volume_percentile_200 > 0.95

Combinations:
    high volume + large return
    high volume + small range
    high volume + large wick
    high volume + close near high/low
    high volume + failed breakout
```

## 8.5 Volume-range divergence anomalies

These are OHLCV-only approximations of absorption or inefficiency. They do not prove order-book absorption, but they identify cases where a lot of volume created little price displacement.

```text
Absorption-like condition:
    volume_percentile_200 > 0.90
    range_percentile_200 < 0.40
    body_ratio < 0.30

Targets:
    future_range_5, future_range_10, fwd_abs_r10

Interpretation:
    High participation with limited displacement may precede expansion or reversal.
```

## 8.6 Wick rejection anomalies

Large wicks show that the bar traded away from the body and returned. Since OHLC does not reveal the exact intrabar path sequence, wick logic must be tested conservatively.

```text
Upper wick rejection:
    upper_wick_ratio > 0.60

Lower wick rejection:
    lower_wick_ratio > 0.60

High-volume wick rejection:
    upper_wick_ratio > 0.50 and volume_z > 1.5
    lower_wick_ratio > 0.50 and volume_z > 1.5

Targets:
    fwd_r1, fwd_r3, fwd_r5, MFE/MAE
```

## 8.7 Close-location anomalies

Close location measures whether the bar closed near its high, low, or middle. It is one of the simplest ways to separate large-range continuation candidates from rejection candidates.

```text
Close near high:
    clv > 0.8

Close near low:
    clv < -0.8

Close near middle:
    abs(clv) < 0.2

Useful combinations:
    large range + clv > 0.8
    large volume + clv < -0.8
    breakout + clv confirmation
```

## 8.8 Breakout anomalies

Breakouts test whether price leaving a recent range has directional continuation or mean reversion. Always compute rolling highs/lows from prior bars only.

```text
Prior rolling levels:
    rolling_high_n = max(H_{t-n},...,H_{t-1})
    rolling_low_n  = min(L_{t-n},...,L_{t-1})

Upside breakout:
    C_t > rolling_high_n

Downside breakout:
    C_t < rolling_low_n

Breakout strength:
    ln(C_t / rolling_high_n) / ATR_log_n
```

## 8.9 Failed breakout anomalies

Failed breakouts are often cleaner than raw breakouts because they identify a rejected attempt to leave a range. They are especially worth testing in choppy regimes.

```text
Failed upside breakout:
    H_t > rolling_high_n and C_t < rolling_high_n

Failed downside breakout:
    L_t < rolling_low_n and C_t > rolling_low_n

Filters:
    volume_z > 1
    efficiency_20 < 0.3
    range_z > 1
```

## 8.10 Gap anomalies

Gaps only exist if the data has discontinuities between prior close and current open. Do not assume daily sessions; simply measure the open-to-previous-close jump.

```text
gap_t = ln(O_t / C_{t-1})

gap_z = zscore(gap_t, prior n)

Tests:
    gap continuation
    gap fill
    gap reversal
    gap + volume
    gap + range expansion
```

## 8.11 Autocorrelation and run anomalies

Return autocorrelation can indicate short-horizon momentum or reversal; absolute-return autocorrelation can indicate volatility clustering. Run features detect unusual sign clustering.

```text
Momentum-style condition:
    autocorr(r1, lag=1, prior 100) > threshold

Reversal-style condition:
    autocorr(r1, lag=1, prior 100) < -threshold

Run anomaly:
    consecutive_up_bars >= k
    consecutive_down_bars >= k

Targets:
    fwd_r_h, hit_rate, barrier-first probability
```

# 9. Regime detection using only OHLCV

Alpha often disappears when all states are mixed together. A condition that works in a choppy, low-volatility regime may fail in a high-volatility trend. Regimes must be defined using only current and prior OHLCV data.

| Regime | Feature | Example split | Why it matters |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Volatility | ATR\_20 / ATR\_100 or ATR percentile | <0.75 low, 0.75-1.25 normal, >1.25 high | Breakout and reversal behavior often changes with volatility. |
| Trend | ln(C\_t/C\_{t-n}) / ATR\_n | >+1.5 uptrend, <-1.5 downtrend | Return shocks may continue in trends and reverse in chop. |
| Chop/efficiency | abs(sum r)/sum(abs r) | >0.6 directional, <0.2 choppy | Failed breakouts and mean reversion often prefer chop. |
| Volume | volume percentile | <0.2 dead, >0.8 active, >0.95 extreme | Participation can confirm or invalidate moves. |
| Compression | ATR\_short / ATR\_long | <0.6 compressed, >1.4 expanded | Compression can precede volatility expansion. |
| Range shape | body\_ratio, wick ratios, CLV | body dominated vs wick dominated | Separates acceptance from rejection candles. |

## 9.1 Regime split protocol

1. Define the regime using rolling features based only on past/current data.
1. Compute baseline target behavior inside each regime, not only globally.
1. Evaluate the anomaly inside each regime.
1. Compare event edge to the regime baseline, not merely to the full-sample baseline.
1. Reject results where the edge comes from one rare regime with too few events unless more data confirms it.

```text
Example:
    baseline_high_vol = mean(fwd_r5 where vol_regime == 'high')
    event_high_vol    = mean(fwd_r5 where signal and vol_regime == 'high')
    regime_edge       = event_high_vol - baseline_high_vol

Do not compare a high-volatility signal only to the all-sample baseline,
because the all-sample baseline may mix incompatible states.
```

# 10. Forward targets, labels, MFE/MAE, and triple-barrier logic

Targets are future quantities used for research. They are not features. Keep target construction strictly separate from signal construction.

| Target | Formula | Question answered |
| --- | --- | --- |
| fwd\_r\_h | ln(C\_{t+h}/C\_t) | Where did price close after h bars? |
| fwd\_abs\_r\_h | abs(fwd\_r\_h) | Was there movement regardless of direction? |
| future\_upside\_h | ln(max(H\_{t+1..t+h}) / C\_t) | How far could a long have gone favorably? |
| future\_downside\_h | ln(C\_t / min(L\_{t+1..t+h})) | How far could a long have gone adversely? |
| future\_range\_h | future\_upside\_h + future\_downside\_h | How large was the path range? |
| MFE\_long\_h | ln(max(H\_{future})/entry) | Maximum favorable excursion for long. |
| MAE\_long\_h | ln(entry/min(L\_{future})) | Maximum adverse excursion for long. |
| barrier\_first | up, down, neither, ambiguous | Which threshold was touched first? |

## 10.1 Bar-level ambiguity

OHLC bars do not reveal the exact intrabar order of high and low. If both a take-profit and stop-loss level are inside the same future bar, OHLC alone cannot prove which one occurred first. This is not a minor detail; it can completely change backtest results.

| Ambiguous case policy | Description | Research stance |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Pessimistic | Assume the adverse barrier was hit first. | Best conservative default. |
| Skip ambiguous | Remove events where both barriers are touched in same bar. | Good for clean inference but may bias sample. |
| Close-based exit only | Ignore intrabar barriers and use close at horizon. | Simple but misses path risk. |
| Optimistic | Assume favorable barrier was hit first. | Do not use for final validation. |

## 10.2 Triple-barrier framework

A path-based test asks whether a signal is tradeable with a stop, target, and maximum holding time. This idea is commonly associated with financial machine-learning labeling; here it is used as a non-ML research tool to evaluate path quality.

```python
At signal bar t:
    entry = C_t
    upper = entry * exp(+tp_atr * ATR_log_n)
    lower = entry * exp(-sl_atr * ATR_log_n)
    vertical_barrier = t + h

Long event result:
    +1 if upper touched first
    -1 if lower touched first
     0 if neither touched before h, then exit at C_{t+h}

Short event result swaps upper/lower interpretation.
```

Because the framework is instrument-agnostic, barriers should usually be scaled by ATR or recent volatility rather than fixed price units.

# 11. Event-study engine and conditional alpha tables

The event study is the core research tool. It converts a feature idea into evidence. For each condition and horizon, it compares future outcomes after the condition against baseline future outcomes.

| Metric | Formula or definition | Reason |
| --- | --- | --- |
| n\_events | count of valid events | Sample size and reliability. |
| event\_mean | mean(target \\| condition) | Average conditional outcome. |
| baseline\_mean | mean(target) | Unconditional comparison. |
| edge | event\_mean - baseline\_mean | Incremental conditional expectation. |
| median | median(target \\| condition) | Robust central tendency. |
| hit\_rate | mean(target > 0) | Directional frequency. |
| avg\_win/avg\_loss | mean positives / mean negatives | Payoff asymmetry. |
| profit\_factor | sum wins / abs(sum losses) | Simple return-quality measure. |
| p05/p95 | 5th and 95th percentiles | Tail behavior. |
| MFE/MAE ratio | mean MFE / mean MAE | Path quality. |
| vol\_lift | mean(abs target \\| signal)/mean(abs target) | Volatility alpha. |
| cost\_break\_even | mean gross return in bps | How much cost can the edge absorb? |

## 11.1 Event de-duplication

If a signal fires on every bar during a regime, naive event studies overcount nearly identical events. For a target horizon h, use a cooldown of at least h bars as a first pass. This creates more independent events and makes the sample less misleading.

```python
def dedupe_events(signal, cooldown):
    events = []
    next_allowed = 0
    for i, flag in enumerate(signal):
        if i < next_allowed:
            continue
        if flag:
            events.append(i)
            next_allowed = i + cooldown
    return events
```

## 11.2 Conditional matrix analysis

Some effects appear only through interactions. A conditional matrix bins two features and computes future outcomes in each cell. This is a beginner-friendly alternative to jumping into machine learning.

```text
Example matrix:
    rows:    return_z bucket (<-2, -2..-1, -1..1, 1..2, >2)
    columns: volume_z bucket (<-1, -1..1, 1..2, >2)
    cell:    count, mean fwd_r5, median fwd_r5, vol_lift

Look for:
    cells with enough count,
    smooth changes across buckets,
    sign-consistent edge,
    stable behavior in validation/test.
```

# 12. Statistical inference: dependence, tails, and uncertainty

Financial event returns are usually not clean independent normal observations. Overlapping horizons, clustered signals, volatility regimes, and fat tails can inflate naive confidence. Treat simple t-statistics as a first diagnostic, not final proof.

## 12.1 Simple t-stat and its limits

```text
sample_mean = mean(signal_returns)
sample_std  = std(signal_returns, ddof=1)
n           = len(signal_returns)
t_stat      = sample_mean / (sample_std / sqrt(n))
```

The simple t-stat assumes independence and finite stable variance. It is useful for ranking candidates, but it should be supplemented with de-duplication, non-overlapping samples, block bootstrap, or HAC-style standard errors. Lo showed that Sharpe-ratio inference changes under different assumptions, especially when returns are serially correlated [7].

## 12.2 HAC/Newey-West logic

When event returns are serially correlated, the standard error of the mean should account for autocorrelation and heteroskedasticity. Newey-West style HAC covariance estimation is a classic tool for this issue [8]. In beginner terms: if returns are clustered, your effective sample size is smaller than the raw event count.

```text
Practical rule:
    If using overlapping fwd_r_h on every bar, naive t-stats are inflated.
    Prefer event cooldown >= h.
    If you still use overlapping samples, report HAC-adjusted t-stats or treat results as exploratory.
```

## 12.3 Bootstrap and stationary bootstrap

Ordinary bootstrap resamples individual observations and can destroy time dependence. Block or stationary bootstrap resamples chunks, preserving some serial structure. Politis and Romano introduced the stationary bootstrap as a way to construct uncertainty estimates for weakly dependent stationary time series [9].

```text
Bootstrap usage in this framework:
    1. Collect de-duplicated signal event returns.
    2. Resample blocks or stationary blocks.
    3. Recompute mean edge, profit factor, and hit rate.
    4. Use confidence intervals, not just point estimates.

Reject candidates whose bootstrap confidence interval is mostly below zero.
```

## 12.4 Base-rate and null-model controls

Every event result should be compared against a relevant baseline. A signal that fires only in high-volatility regimes should be compared to the high-volatility regime baseline, not only the all-sample baseline.

- Unconditional baseline: all bars.
- Regime baseline: bars in the same regime as the signal.
- Random-event baseline: random timestamps with same count and cooldown.
- Sign-shuffle or block-shuffle baseline: preserves some distributional structure while breaking the specific condition.
- Cost-adjusted baseline: gross edge minus cost grid.

# 13. Multiple testing, data snooping, and backtest overfitting controls

If you test 1,000 conditions, some will look good by chance. This is not a moral failure; it is arithmetic. The correct response is to design the research process so false positives are less likely to survive.

White described the problem of data snooping when the same dataset is reused for inference and model selection [3]. Hansen proposed a Superior Predictive Ability test that improves on earlier reality-check ideas in some settings [4]. Benjamini and Hochberg introduced false discovery rate control for multiple testing [10]. Bailey and coauthors emphasized that backtests can be overfit even when standard holdouts are used carelessly [5].

## 13.1 Beginner multiple-testing discipline

1. Keep a research log of every tested condition, threshold, horizon, and regime split.
1. Separate feature discovery from final validation.
1. Do not repeatedly inspect the final test set.
1. Prefer simple round thresholds and smooth robustness regions.
1. Treat every threshold sweep as multiple tests, not one test.
1. Use FDR, Reality Check, SPA, Deflated Sharpe Ratio, or at least more conservative acceptance criteria when many trials are run.
1. Require economic significance after costs, not just statistical significance.

## 13.2 Trial log template

| Field | What to record |
| --- | --- |
| trial\_id | Unique identifier. |
| date\_researched | When the trial was first run. |
| condition | Exact signal formula. |
| features\_used | All feature names and lookbacks. |
| thresholds | All thresholds tested, not only winner. |
| horizons | Forward targets tested. |
| regimes | All regime filters tested. |
| train\_result | Metrics on training segment. |
| validation\_result | Metrics on validation segment. |
| test\_result | Filled only once, after freezing rule. |
| verdict | Reject, watchlist, volatility alpha, directional alpha, or needs more data. |

## 13.3 Deflated performance thinking

The Deflated Sharpe Ratio is one formal way to correct for selection bias, multiple testing, and non-normality [6]. A beginner does not need to implement the full formula immediately, but should internalize its logic: the best result among many trials must clear a higher bar than the result of one pre-registered test.

```text
Mental model:
    If you tested 1 rule and it has Sharpe 1.2, that is one situation.
    If you tested 5,000 variations and the best has Sharpe 1.2, that is weaker evidence.

The number of hidden trials matters.
```

# 14. Train/validation/test, walk-forward, purging, and embargoing

Time series must be split by time, not randomly shuffled. Random splits leak future regimes into training and create overly optimistic results. Scikit-learn documents TimeSeriesSplit as a way to split time-ordered data where ordinary cross-validation would be inappropriate [17]. For event labels with overlapping future horizons, add purging or embargo logic so training labels do not overlap test labels.

## 14.1 Simple split

```text
Example:
    train:      first 60% of bars
    validation: next 20% of bars
    test:       final 20% of bars

Use:
    train      -> discover features and rough thresholds
    validation -> tune simple threshold choices
    test       -> one final frozen evaluation
```

## 14.2 Walk-forward split

Walk-forward validation repeatedly trains on a historical window and tests on the next block. It checks whether the anomaly survives across different periods instead of depending on one lucky split.

```python
for each fold:
    train = bars [start : start + train_len]
    test  = bars [start + train_len + gap : start + train_len + gap + test_len]
    evaluate frozen logic or tune only within train
    record out-of-sample metrics

After all folds:
    inspect distribution of fold returns, hit rates, and drawdowns.
```

## 14.3 Purging and embargo logic

If a label at time t uses future bars up to t+h, then samples near the test window can overlap with training labels. Purging removes training samples whose label windows overlap the test label windows. Embargoing removes a buffer after the test set. This logic is emphasized in financial machine-learning validation because standard cross-validation can leak information when labels overlap in time [18].

```text
For each event i:
    feature_time_i = t_i
    label_start_i  = t_i + 1
    label_end_i    = t_i + h

When test events occupy [a,b], remove train events whose label interval intersects [a,b].
Then optionally embargo k bars after b.
```

# 15. Cost and slippage stress without instrument assumptions

Because the framework cannot assume a specific instrument, do not use one fixed transaction-cost number. Instead, report performance across a cost grid. The goal is to understand how much cost the gross edge can absorb.

```python
cost_grid_bps = [0, 1, 2, 5, 10, 20, 50]

For one round-trip trade:
    net_return = gross_return - 2 * cost_bps / 10000

Break-even one-way cost approximation:
    break_even_bps = mean_gross_return * 10000 / 2
```

| Signal average gross return | Round-trip break-even cost | Interpretation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 0.0002 = 2 bps | 1 bp one-way | Extremely cost-sensitive. |
| 0.0008 = 8 bps | 4 bps one-way | May survive liquid low-cost execution only. |
| 0.0020 = 20 bps | 10 bps one-way | More robust but still needs slippage stress. |
| Volatility-only signal | Not directly comparable | Must evaluate in the final strategy context. |

Also test delayed execution. A signal computed at the close may not be fillable at that same close. Instrument-agnostic research should compare close-to-close theoretical results against next-open or next-bar-close execution variants when those prices are available.

# 16. Rule construction, ranking, and alpha scorecards

Only convert an anomaly into a candidate rule after it has evidence. The rule should be simple enough to explain and robust enough to survive reasonable parameter changes.

| Gate | Minimum standard |
| --- | --- |

| Out-of-sample edge | Positive after reasonable costs on validation and test. |

# 18. Full research-engine pseudocode and Python skeleton

The following skeleton is designed to be readable and auditable. It is not optimized for speed. It shows the separation between cleaning, feature generation, target generation, event testing, de-duplication, and threshold sweeps.

```python
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd

EPS = 1e-12

def safe_log_ratio(a, b):
    return np.log(np.maximum(a, EPS) / np.maximum(b, EPS))

def rolling_zscore_past(x, n):
    mu = x.shift(1).rolling(n).mean()
    sd = x.shift(1).rolling(n).std(ddof=1)
    return (x - mu) / sd.replace(0, np.nan)

def rolling_robust_z_past(x, n):
    med = x.shift(1).rolling(n).median()
    def mad(arr):
        m = np.nanmedian(arr)
        return np.nanmedian(np.abs(arr - m))
    madv = x.shift(1).rolling(n).apply(mad, raw=True)
    return 0.6745 * (x - med) / madv.replace(0, np.nan)

def rolling_percentile_past(x, n):
    def pct_rank(arr):
        current = arr[-1]
        hist = arr[:-1]
        hist = hist[~np.isnan(hist)]
        if len(hist) == 0 or np.isnan(current):
            return np.nan
        return np.mean(hist <= current)
    return x.rolling(n + 1).apply(pct_rank, raw=True)

def add_base_features(df):
    df = df.copy()
    O,H,L,C,V = df['open'], df['high'], df['low'], df['close'], df['volume']

    df['r1'] = safe_log_ratio(C, C.shift(1))
    for n in [2,3,5,10,20,50,100]:
        df[f'r{n}'] = safe_log_ratio(C, C.shift(n))

    df['oc'] = safe_log_ratio(C, O)
    df['gap'] = safe_log_ratio(O, C.shift(1))
    df['hl_range'] = safe_log_ratio(H, L)
    df['body'] = np.abs(df['oc'])
    df['upper_wick'] = safe_log_ratio(H, np.maximum(O, C))
    df['lower_wick'] = safe_log_ratio(np.minimum(O, C), L)

    rng = (H - L).replace(0, np.nan)
    df['clv'] = ((2*C - H - L) / rng).fillna(0.0)
    df['body_ratio'] = df['body'] / df['hl_range'].replace(0, np.nan)
    df['upper_wick_ratio'] = df['upper_wick'] / df['hl_range'].replace(0, np.nan)
    df['lower_wick_ratio'] = df['lower_wick'] / df['hl_range'].replace(0, np.nan)
    df['wick_imbalance'] = (df['upper_wick'] - df['lower_wick']) / df['hl_range'].replace(0, np.nan)

    tr1 = safe_log_ratio(H, L)
    tr2 = np.abs(safe_log_ratio(H, C.shift(1)))
    tr3 = np.abs(safe_log_ratio(L, C.shift(1)))
    df['true_range_log'] = np.maximum.reduce([tr1, tr2, tr3])

    df['log_volume'] = np.log1p(V)
    df['notional_proxy'] = C * V
    df['log_notional_proxy'] = np.log1p(df['notional_proxy'])

    for n in [20,50,100,200]:
        df[f'r1_z_{n}'] = rolling_zscore_past(df['r1'], n)
        df[f'range_z_{n}'] = rolling_zscore_past(df['hl_range'], n)
        df[f'volume_z_{n}'] = rolling_zscore_past(df['log_volume'], n)
        df[f'range_pct_{n}'] = rolling_percentile_past(df['hl_range'], n)
        df[f'volume_pct_{n}'] = rolling_percentile_past(df['log_volume'], n)
        df[f'atr_log_{n}'] = df['true_range_log'].shift(1).rolling(n).mean()

    df['atr_ratio_20_100'] = df['atr_log_20'] / df['atr_log_100']
    df['trend_score_20'] = df['r20'] / df['atr_log_20'].replace(0, np.nan)

    path = df['r1'].abs().shift(1).rolling(20).sum()
    net = df['r1'].shift(1).rolling(20).sum().abs()
    df['efficiency_20'] = net / path.replace(0, np.nan)

    df['rolling_high_20'] = H.shift(1).rolling(20).max()
    df['rolling_low_20']  = L.shift(1).rolling(20).min()
    return df

def add_forward_targets(df, horizons=(1,3,5,10,20)):
    df = df.copy()
    C,H,L = df['close'], df['high'], df['low']
    for h in horizons:
        df[f'fwd_r{h}'] = safe_log_ratio(C.shift(-h), C)
        df[f'fwd_abs_r{h}'] = df[f'fwd_r{h}'].abs()
        fut_high = H.shift(-1).rolling(h).max().shift(-(h-1))
        fut_low  = L.shift(-1).rolling(h).min().shift(-(h-1))
        df[f'future_upside_{h}'] = safe_log_ratio(fut_high, C)
        df[f'future_downside_{h}'] = safe_log_ratio(C, fut_low)
        df[f'future_range_{h}'] = df[f'future_upside_{h}'] + df[f'future_downside_{h}']
    return df

def event_study(df, signal, target, cooldown=None):
    sig = signal.fillna(False).values
    if cooldown is None:
        idx = np.flatnonzero(sig)
    else:
        idx = []
        next_allowed = 0
        for i, flag in enumerate(sig):
            if i < next_allowed:
                continue
            if flag:
                idx.append(i)
                next_allowed = i + cooldown
        idx = np.array(idx, dtype=int)

    event = df.iloc[idx][target].dropna()
    base = df[target].dropna()
    wins = event[event > 0]
    losses = event[event < 0]

    return {
        'n': int(event.shape[0]),
        'event_mean': event.mean(),
        'baseline_mean': base.mean(),
        'edge': event.mean() - base.mean(),
        'median': event.median(),
        'hit_rate': (event > 0).mean(),
        'avg_win': wins.mean(),
        'avg_loss': losses.mean(),
        'profit_factor': wins.sum() / abs(losses.sum()) if losses.sum() != 0 else np.nan,
        'p05': event.quantile(0.05),
        'p95': event.quantile(0.95),
        't_stat_simple': event.mean() / (event.std(ddof=1) / np.sqrt(len(event))) if len(event) > 2 else np.nan,
    }
```

## 18.1 Triple-barrier pseudocode

```python
def triple_barrier_event(df, i, horizon, atr_col, tp_atr=1.0, sl_atr=1.0, side='long', ambiguous='pessimistic'):
    entry = df['close'].iloc[i]
    atr = df[atr_col].iloc[i]
    if pd.isna(entry) or pd.isna(atr) or atr <= 0:
        return np.nan

    upper = entry * np.exp(tp_atr * atr)
    lower = entry * np.exp(-sl_atr * atr)

    for j in range(i+1, min(i+horizon+1, len(df))):
        hi = df['high'].iloc[j]
        lo = df['low'].iloc[j]
        hit_up = hi >= upper
        hit_dn = lo <= lower

        if hit_up and hit_dn:
            if ambiguous == 'skip':
                return np.nan
            if ambiguous == 'pessimistic':
                return -1 if side == 'long' else -1
            if ambiguous == 'optimistic':
                return +1

        if side == 'long':
            if hit_up: return +1
            if hit_dn: return -1
        else:
            if hit_dn: return +1
            if hit_up: return -1

    exit_price = df['close'].iloc[min(i+horizon, len(df)-1)]
    ret = np.log(exit_price / entry)
    return np.sign(ret) if side == 'long' else np.sign(-ret)
```

## 20.2 Beginner checklist before trusting any OHLCV alpha

- Did every feature use only current or past OHLCV data?
- Were rolling highs/lows shifted so breakout levels exclude the current bar?
- Were z-scores and percentiles computed from prior values only?
- Were future targets kept separate from features?
- Were repeated adjacent signals de-duplicated?
- Was the baseline computed globally and inside the relevant regime?
- Did the result survive train, validation, and test periods?
- Did the result survive reasonable threshold changes?
- Did the result survive cost and delayed-entry stress?
- Were ambiguous same-bar barrier hits handled conservatively?
- Was the number of tested ideas recorded?
- Is the final rule simple enough to explain without hindsight?

## 20.3 What to reject immediately

- Any signal using future data, full-sample normalization, or unshifted breakout levels.
- Any result with too few events unless it is explicitly labeled exploratory.
- Any rule that only works at one exact threshold.
- Any edge caused by one or two outlier trades.
- Any rule that is great in-sample and dead out-of-sample.
- Any strategy whose average edge is smaller than minimal plausible transaction costs.
- Any path test that assumes favorable same-bar barrier ordering without proof.
- Any result found after many hidden trials without adjustment or skepticism.

# Appendix A. Expanded feature engineering dictionary

The feature dictionary is organized by what a feature measures. A beginner should not treat this as a menu of magic indicators. Treat it as a controlled vocabulary for describing what happened in the OHLCV stream. Features should be added to a research log, normalized with past-only rolling windows, and tested through event studies before they influence a rule.

## A.1 Lookback design

Use several horizons because OHLCV effects can exist at one scale and disappear at another. A one-bar shock can reverse while a twenty-bar trend can continue. The goal is not to optimize every lookback; the goal is to measure whether an effect is local to a sensible scale family.

| Lookback group | Example values | Purpose | Risk |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Very short | 1, 2, 3 bars | Detect immediate shocks, gaps, and candle reactions. | High noise and high cost sensitivity. |
| Short | 5, 10 bars | Detect local continuation/reversal and short volatility cycles. | Can overlap heavily if targets are also 5-10 bars. |
| Medium | 20, 50 bars | Detect regimes, breakouts, rolling highs/lows, recent trend. | Can lag after sudden regime changes. |
| Long | 100, 200, 500 bars | Normalize slow volume/volatility changes and identify historical extremes. | May be stale in adaptive markets. |
| Multi-scale ratios | 10/100, 20/200 | Compression/expansion and local-vs-background comparison. | Ratios can explode when denominator is near zero. |

## A.3 Feature construction rules

1. Every rolling feature used to judge the current bar should be computed from prior bars unless the current bar itself is the object being measured.
1. Every normalized feature should have a missing-value policy for early bars with insufficient lookback history.
1. Every feature should have a unit test proving it does not use future data.
1. Every feature family should have a reason for being in the library. Do not add features simply because a library exposes them.
1. Features that are algebraically almost identical should be grouped so they do not create an inflated sense of independent evidence.
1. Feature importance from any model is not evidence of alpha unless the entire model and validation process are leak-free and out-of-sample.

# Appendix C. Statistical cookbook for OHLCV event studies

This appendix explains the statistical tools in operational terms. A beginner can start with simple metrics, but should know what each metric assumes and what can go wrong.

| Tool | Basic formula | Use | Caution |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Mean edge | mean(target \\| signal) - mean(target baseline) | First measure of conditional expectation. | Can be dominated by outliers; always inspect median and quantiles. |
| Median edge | median(target \\| signal) - median(target baseline) | Robust central tendency. | Can miss rare large-payoff strategies. |
| Hit-rate edge | P(target>0 \\| signal) - P(target>0 baseline) | Directional frequency. | High hit rate can still lose if losses are larger than wins. |
| Profit factor | sum positive returns / abs(sum negative returns) | Payoff quality. | Can be inflated by small number of large wins. |
| Tail quantiles | p01, p05, p95, p99 | Downside and upside tails. | Needs enough events to estimate reliably. |
| Simple t-stat | mean / standard error | Fast diagnostic. | Assumes weak dependence and stable variance. Use cautiously. |
| HAC t-stat | mean estimate with autocorrelation-robust standard error | Handles serial dependence better. | Still asymptotic and parameter-dependent. |
| Block bootstrap CI | resample blocks; recompute metric | Nonparametric uncertainty with dependence. | Block length choice matters. |
| Permutation test | shuffle or block-shuffle signal labels | Tests whether condition is special. | Must preserve relevant dependence structure. |
| FDR control | adjust p-values across many tests | Controls expected false discovery rate. | Dependent tests complicate interpretation. |
| Reality Check / SPA logic | compare best strategy against data-snooping null | Designed for many strategy comparisons. | More complex to implement correctly. |
| Deflated Sharpe thinking | adjust observed performance for trials and non-normality | Penalizes selection bias. | Requires trial accounting and return distribution estimates. |

## C.1 Outlier audit procedure

1. Sort event returns from worst to best.
1. Compute mean edge with all events.
1. Compute mean edge after removing the single best event.
1. Compute mean edge after removing the top 1% and bottom 1% of events.
1. Compute median and interquartile range.
1. If the conclusion depends on one event, label the anomaly as fragile.

```python
def outlier_audit(event_returns):
    x = event_returns.dropna().sort_values()
    full = x.mean()
    no_best = x.iloc[:-1].mean() if len(x) > 2 else np.nan
    trimmed = x.iloc[int(0.01*len(x)) : int(0.99*len(x))].mean()
    return {'full_mean': full, 'no_best_mean': no_best, 'trimmed_mean': trimmed,
            'median': x.median(), 'p05': x.quantile(.05), 'p95': x.quantile(.95)}
```

# Appendix D. Validation designs and failure modes

| Design | Description | Strength | Weakness |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Single holdout | Train/validation/test split by time. | Simple and understandable. | One split may be lucky or unlucky. |
| Anchored walk-forward | Train starts fixed and expands; test next block. | Uses growing history. | Old data may dominate. |
| Rolling walk-forward | Fixed-size training window rolls forward. | Adapts to changing regimes. | Can discard useful long history. |
| Purged k-fold | Time groups with overlapping label intervals removed. | Reduces leakage from overlapping labels. | More complex; not always needed for simple event studies. |
| Embargoed split | Gap after test block removed from training. | Reduces spillover leakage. | Reduces sample size. |
| Combinatorial purged CV | Many purged train/test combinations. | Produces distribution of OOS outcomes. | Complex; easy to implement wrong. |
| Final untouched test | One segment never used until final decision. | Best guardrail against self-deception. | Consumes data and cannot be reused repeatedly. |

## D.1 Validation failure modes

- Repeated test-set peeking: adjusting the rule after seeing test results turns the test set into validation data.
- Random shuffling: destroys time ordering and leaks future regimes into training.
- Overlapping labels: fwd\_r20 events at consecutive bars share most of their future returns.
- Unshifted normalization: using full-sample or future statistics makes anomalies look cleaner than they were live.
- Survivor rules: only documenting winners hides the true number of trials.
- Regime hindsight: using future performance to define regimes creates false conditional alpha.
- Cost blindness: a gross edge that cannot survive one or two basis points is fragile for many practical contexts.
- Execution fantasy: assuming entry at the exact close that created the signal may be unrealistic unless the use case permits it.

# Appendix L. Deep reasoning: why each part of the framework exists

A beginner often wants a list of indicators. That is backwards. The purpose of this appendix is to explain the logic underneath the framework so the user can reason about new OHLCV situations without memorizing patterns. The key idea is that every bar contains only a few primitive facts: how far price moved, where it closed inside its range, how much range occurred, how much volume occurred, and how those facts compare with the recent past. Everything else is a transformation of those facts.

## L.1 Why log returns are the default

Raw price differences are not scale invariant. A 1 point move is enormous for a 2 unit instrument and tiny for a 2,000 unit instrument. Log returns convert price movement into proportional movement, which makes features more comparable across regimes and across unknown instruments.

Log returns are also additive across time: ln(C\_t/C\_{t-1}) + ln(C\_{t+1}/C\_t) = ln(C\_{t+1}/C\_{t-1}). This makes multi-horizon returns easier to reason about and reduces many scaling problems.

This does not mean log returns magically make the data stationary. It only removes the most obvious price-level dependence. You still need rolling normalization, robust statistics, and regime splitting.

## L.2 Why anomalies must be relative to history

A range of 1% can be huge in one regime and normal in another. A volume print of 1,000 units can be massive in one dataset and irrelevant in another. Since the framework does not assume an instrument, every anomaly must be measured relative to the instrument own past.

This is why rolling z-scores, robust z-scores, percentiles, and ratios to rolling medians are core tools. They transform raw OHLCV values into statements like: this bar is larger than 95% of the prior 200 bars, or this volume is 2.5 robust standard deviations above its past median.

The current bar should usually be compared to prior history, not to future history. Full-sample statistics are convenient for analysis but invalid for live signal construction.

## L.3 Why a signal can be useful without predicting direction

Many OHLCV anomalies are better at predicting movement magnitude than direction. Range compression, volume/range divergence, and absolute-return shocks can increase the probability of larger future movement while leaving the expected future return near zero.

A beginner may discard these because the average future return is flat. That is a mistake. Volatility alpha can be used to time breakout triggers, adjust risk, avoid dead zones, or decide when a directional rule should be allowed to operate.

Therefore every event study should include fwd\_abs\_return, future\_range, MFE, MAE, and barrier-first outcomes, not only fwd\_return.

## L.4 Why regimes are not optional

Markets are not one behavior repeated forever. Even without knowing the instrument, the OHLCV stream can show high volatility, low volatility, trend, chop, compression, expansion, high volume, and low volume. These states can completely change how the same anomaly behaves.

For example, a large positive return can continue in a high-efficiency trend and reverse in a choppy range. A failed breakout can be powerful in chop and dangerous in a strong trend. A high-volume close near the high can be continuation in one regime and exhaustion in another.

The goal of regime splitting is not to create endless filters. The goal is to avoid averaging incompatible states and then concluding that no edge exists.

## L.5 Why volume does not reveal buyer or seller identity

OHLCV volume says how much traded, not who initiated the trades, what the order book looked like, or whether institutions were buying or selling. Without order book or trade classification data, volume must be interpreted only as participation intensity.

This is why the document uses phrases like high participation, volume shock, and volume/range divergence instead of claiming true absorption, accumulation, or distribution. Those stronger claims require data outside OHLCV.

However, participation intensity can still be useful. If abnormal volume coincides with a close near the high, a failed breakout, a long wick, or a tiny range, the future distribution may change. That is testable without pretending to know hidden order flow.

## L.6 Why wicks are useful but dangerous

A wick means the bar high or low extended away from the body. It does not reveal the exact sequence of trades inside the bar. A bar with a long lower wick and close near the high might have dropped first and recovered, or it might have rallied first and then briefly dropped. OHLC alone cannot always tell.

For candle-shape research, this ambiguity is acceptable if the target is close-to-close return. It becomes dangerous for stop-loss and take-profit testing, where the order of high and low matters. If both barriers are touched in the same bar, the event must be marked ambiguous or handled pessimistically.

This one rule prevents a large class of fake backtests.

## L.7 Why breakouts must exclude the current bar

A common beginner bug is computing rolling\_high\_20 = max(high over the last 20 bars including the current bar), and then testing whether close\_t > rolling\_high\_20. That condition is usually impossible or distorted because the current high is already included.

The correct level is max(high from t-20 through t-1). At the close of bar t, you can know whether the current close exceeded the prior range. This small shift is one of the most important anti-lookahead habits.

The same logic applies to rolling lows, volatility baselines, percentile ranks, and any reference level that is supposed to represent prior history.

## L.8 Why average return is not enough

A positive mean can hide a negative median and one huge outlier. A high hit rate can hide rare catastrophic losses. A good close-to-close result can hide terrible intrabar adverse excursion. A beautiful in-sample equity curve can be the best curve among thousands of failed trials.

For every candidate, inspect mean, median, hit rate, average win, average loss, profit factor, p05, p95, MFE, MAE, drawdown, and cost sensitivity. The purpose is not to drown in metrics; it is to understand what kind of edge, if any, exists.

A robust edge is usually visible from more than one angle.

## L.9 Why cost stress is done after gross anomaly discovery

If you apply a guessed transaction cost too early, you may discard a real statistical anomaly that could be useful in a different execution context. If you ignore costs completely, you may keep signals that cannot possibly be traded.

The instrument-agnostic solution is to first identify gross conditional structure, then report a cost grid. The break-even cost becomes a property of the anomaly: this signal can absorb about X basis points before the mean edge disappears.

This keeps the framework honest without assuming a specific market microstructure.

## L.10 Why the research log matters

The most dangerous backtest is not the one that loses. It is the one that wins after you forgot the 500 variations that failed. Every threshold, feature combination, and horizon tested increases the chance that one result looks good by accident.

A research log is the simplest defense. It forces you to remember how many opportunities noise had to fool you. It also makes final reports more trustworthy because rejected ideas remain visible.

This is the practical beginner version of data-snooping discipline.

# Appendix M. Decision trees for interpreting event-study results

An event table can be confusing when metrics disagree. Use the decision trees below to decide what to check next. These are not absolute laws; they are debugging logic.

| Observation | Likely meaning | Next diagnostic |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Mean positive, median negative | A few large winners may dominate. | Run outlier audit, inspect p95, remove top event, check profit factor. |
| Hit rate high, profit factor low | Wins are frequent but too small. | Check average win/loss, costs, and stop/target asymmetry. |
| Profit factor high, event count low | Could be sample luck. | Require more data, bootstrap, and OOS confirmation. |
| Train great, validation weak | Likely overfit or regime-specific. | Simplify rule, test regimes, avoid touching test set. |
| Global weak, one regime strong | Possible regime-specific alpha. | Compare to regime baseline and require enough events. |
| Direction flat, abs return high | Volatility alpha candidate. | Test future\_range, barrier movement, and breakout trigger. |
| Close-to-close good, MFE/MAE bad | Path is painful or untradeable. | Change exit logic or reject as strategy candidate. |
| Edge disappears after cooldown | Signal clustering inflated evidence. | Use de-duplicated events as primary result. |
| Edge disappears after delayed entry | Entry assumption unrealistic or edge decays fast. | Report as non-actionable unless execution supports it. |
| Edge disappears after tiny cost | Too cost-sensitive. | Reject for practical trading; may remain research signal. |
| Threshold works only at one value | Curve fitting likely. | Reject or keep exploratory until OOS confirms. |
| Strong mean caused by one period | Regime or sample dependency. | Walk-forward and period contribution analysis. |

# Appendix N. Advanced non-ML analyses that still respect OHLCV-only constraints

Before using machine learning, a researcher can do a large amount of serious statistical work with simple tables. These methods are interpretable and less likely to hide leakage.

| Method | How it works | What it reveals |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Quantile sorting | Sort a feature into deciles using past-only ranks and compute future target mean in each decile. | Look for monotonic behavior or extreme-tail behavior. |
| Two-way heatmaps | Bucket two features, such as return\_z and volume\_z, then compute event outcomes by cell. | Find interactions without fitting a black-box model. |
| Regime-normalized edge | Subtract the baseline return of the same regime from each event outcome. | Avoid mistaking regime drift for signal edge. |
| Conditional volatility lift | Compute mean future\_abs\_return after signal divided by baseline future\_abs\_return. | Identify volatility alpha. |
| Barrier-first probability | Measure whether up or down ATR barriers are touched first. | Convert close prediction into path prediction. |
| Rank information coefficient | Spearman correlation between feature value and future return/abs return. | Check whether a continuous feature has predictive ordering. |
| Monotonicity check | Count how often higher feature bins produce higher target means. | Reject jagged relationships likely caused by noise. |
| Subperiod contribution | Compute edge by month/quarter/block. | Find whether one period creates all performance. |
| Null event sampling | Randomly sample timestamps with same cooldown and count as signal. | Compare discovered signal to random events. |
| Block permutation | Shuffle blocks or signs to break specific condition while preserving dependence. | Estimate how surprising the edge is under a time-dependent null. |

## N.1 Quantile-sort interpretation

Quantile sorting is one of the best beginner tools. Instead of choosing a threshold first, sort a feature into 5 or 10 buckets and compute future outcomes in each bucket. If only one bucket has a huge result and neighboring buckets do not, the effect may be noise. If the target changes smoothly across buckets, the feature may contain real information.

```python
Example quantile research:
    feature = volume_z_200
    buckets = deciles based on rolling or training distribution
    target = fwd_abs_r10

Expected robust pattern for volatility alpha:
    higher volume_z buckets generally have higher future_abs_return,
    top bucket has strongest lift,
    pattern repeats in validation/test.
```

## N.2 Interaction heatmap interpretation

A feature can look useless alone and useful in combination. For example, return\_z may not predict direction globally, and volume\_z may not predict direction globally, but the cell where return\_z is strongly positive and volume\_z is also high may show continuation. Conversely, a strong return on low volume may reverse. The heatmap makes this visible without imposing a complex model.

```text
Example interaction:
    rows    = return_z buckets
    columns = volume_z buckets
    cell    = mean(fwd_r5), median(fwd_r5), n_events

Do not trust cells with tiny counts.
Do not trust isolated cells that do not repeat out-of-sample.
Do not convert the best cell into a strategy until thresholds are frozen and validated.
```

# Appendix P. Practical rule simplification guide

Beginners often build rules by adding filters until the backtest improves. This is dangerous. Every added filter reduces sample size and increases multiple-testing burden. Use the simplification guide below before accepting a candidate.

1. Start with the single strongest condition and record its event study.
1. Add one filter only if it has a clear reason and improves validation, not only training.
1. Compare the two-condition rule against each single condition separately.
1. Reject filters that reduce event count sharply without improving out-of-sample edge.
1. Avoid filters that use highly redundant information unless they improve path risk.
1. Prefer percentile thresholds over oddly specific numeric thresholds.
1. Stop at 2 or 3 conditions unless there is a strong research reason.
1. Write the rule in one sentence. If the sentence is impossible to understand, the rule is probably overfit.

> **Simplification test**
> A robust OHLCV anomaly should usually be explainable as: when feature A is unusually high/low, in regime B, future target C changes. If the rule needs 12 clauses, treat it as a fragile fit until proven otherwise.

## 20.5 Closing summary

The correct OHLCV alpha workflow is not indicator hunting. It is anomaly discovery under strict information rules, followed by conditional distribution testing, regime decomposition, robust inference, multiple-testing control, path analysis, and cost stress. The beginner who learns this workflow will avoid most common backtest traps and will be able to turn raw OHLCV bars into a disciplined research process.

```text
Final research pipeline:
    OHLCV
    -> clean and validate
    -> log returns, ranges, volume transforms
    -> rolling normalization using only past data
    -> anomaly conditions
    -> forward targets
    -> event studies
    -> regime splits
    -> threshold robustness
    -> walk-forward validation
    -> multiple-testing adjustment or conservative acceptance
    -> cost and path stress
    -> candidate rule dossier
    -> final untouched test result
```
